[2] Description: New research shows how data centres could become water-positive and carbon-negative, by using waste heat to power water purification and carbon capture ГўВҖВ“ potentially reducing the environmental footprint of AI.
[2] Published Time: 2026-03-30T14:00:00+02:00
[2] Date: Mar 30, 2026

The push to advance artificial intelligence (AI) is driving unprecedented growth in global data centre development ГўВҖВ“ and with it, a surge in energy demand, carbon emissions and water consumption. By 2030 data centres could consume 3-4% of global electricity and, by 2027, around 5 billion cubic metres of water annually. As EuropeГўВҖВҷs AI infrastructure expands rapidly, this is expected to create major challenges for decisionmakers balancing technological competitiveness with environmental and societal resilience.

Efforts to reduce energy consumption and to develop advanced cooling technologies are underway ГўВҖВ“ which has enabled greater amounts of waste heat to be produced by these centres. New analysis has proposed a way to rethink data centresГўВҖВҷ role as passive energy consumers and instead use the waste heat to capture carbon and water from the air, or to thermally purify water. This analysis identifies pathways for making data centres ГўВҖВҳcarbon-negativeГўВҖВҷ (removing more carbon dioxide than they emit) and/or ГўВҖВҳwater-positiveГўВҖВҷ (contributing more water than they consume).

Researchers conducted a detailed thermodynamic, economic and emissions analysis of possible end uses for the low-grade waste heat produced by data centres (typically 30-70ГӮВ°C); bearing in mind these uses would need to be geographically flexible. They evaluated six potential applications: district heating; heat-to-electricity conversion; cooling water through sorption chillers; thermal water purification; atmospheric water harvesting; and direct air capture (DAC) of COГўВӮВӮ.

Using moderate projections of U.S. data centre energy use (around 200 terawatt-hours by 2030), they compared the carbon emissions benefit and economic benefit of each approach, factoring in efficiency and product value.

Their results showed that two end uses were most promising in both positive climate impacts and economic potential:

*   direct air capture (DAC) powered by waste heat, which could remove some 50-1,000 megatonnes of COГўВӮВӮ annually; and
*   thermal water purification, which uses the same heat to convert seawater or brackish ground water into potable water, turning data centres into net water producers.

Both applications outperform conventional heat recovery (such as space heating) in carbon impacts and economic viability ГўВҖВ“ with their geographic flexibility giving an advantage compared to other options. DAC, in particular, the researchers suggest, could generate up to $100 billion USD (ГўВӮВ¬85.6bn) annually in economic value while contributing significantly to net-zero goals. The researchers also propose a metric, Energy Utilisation Efficiency+ (EUE+), which aims to emphasise capturing as much benefit as possible from data centre electricity, and which focuses on _useful_ computational work.

Some challenges are yet to be addressed. For example, the waste heat produced may be at temperatures too low to ensure high performance from DAC systems, as the chips need to be kept cool enough to be reliable. The variability of the heat produced may need careful matching with DAC systems, and the magnitude of heat produced may also pose some challenges - the researchers suggest that using all the heat from a data centre would require a larger DAC facility than any currently demonstrated worldwide. While a recent [study for the European Parliament](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/thinktank/en/document/ECTI_ATA(2025)772477) found that direct air capture could play an enabling role in EU decarbonisation, it also found that the current costs of DAC are high and uncertain. For water purification, mass transfer of the purified water and careful system coordination to ensure safe temperatures for chip operation would also be needed.

The European CommissionГўВҖВҷs [Water Resilience Strategy](https://commission.europa.eu/topics/environment/water-resilience-strategy_en) aims to build a water-smart economy, and proposes measures to promote water savings across data centres. This study highlights how greater AI sustainability and a more circular digital economy could be achieved by treating data centres as a crucial opportunity to use waste heat for water purification, which could then contribute to a more water-smart economy.

The study concludes that, with further innovations in advanced cooling and intelligent heat management, data centres could ГўВҖВҳflip the switchГўВҖВҷ from producing carbon and consuming water to capturing carbon and producing water. Using their assumptions in EUE+, the researchers posit that a single kilowatt-hour of computing energy could simultaneously remove half a kilogram of COГўВӮВӮ and generate half a kilogram of water. The same infrastructure powering EuropeГўВҖВҷs digital transition could also help to power its green transition ГўВҖВ“ if we learn to use every watt for multiple purposes.

November 20, 2025

Across the country, from [Virginia](https://www.vedp.org/industry/data-centers) to [Michigan](https://www.usnews.com/news/best-states/michigan/articles/2025-10-31/tech-giants-announce-7b-data-center-michigans-first-hyperscale-campus) to [Arizona](https://www.govtech.com/artificial-intelligence/two-new-data-centers-may-come-to-tucson-ariz-area), tech companies are pouring billions of dollars into new data center projects needed to store and manage digital information amid the rise of artificial intelligence (AI). Meanwhile, state and local governments are [enticed by projected increases](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/public-sector/our-insights/the-data-center-balance-how-us-states-can-navigate-the-opportunities-and-challenges) in economic output, job creation, property tax revenue, and more that may accompany such projects.
